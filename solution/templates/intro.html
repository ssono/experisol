The Game

What would happen if you had 1,000 people working together to solve a problem? The problem itself doesn’t matter and neither do the people. In fact, let’s imagine that these people have never met and are communicating in a somewhat organized way over the internet. Chances are you’d have 1000 different solutions, 10 times that many arguments, and pure chaos. Now, I’m changing the game a bit. Let’s say that this brainstorming session has a moderator. This moderator’s job is to come up with his or her own solution and then work with the other 999 people to improve it. Depending on how open-minded the moderator is and the helpfulness of the other 999, the outcome could be spectacular.

The ideology, age, sex, race, and economic status of the participants are stripped from their ideas. Disagreements or attacks based on factors that aren’t related to the discussion become impossible because that information isn’t available. 1000 unique ways to view the problem, and therefore 1000 opportunities to make the solution better. If two heads are better than one, how much better are 1000? Unlike other discussions of this kind, the barrier of entry is not a PhD or a political office, but access to the medium over which it occurs. In the process of imagining a solution, everyone has benefitted from new ideas, new relationships, and a stimulating exercise.
